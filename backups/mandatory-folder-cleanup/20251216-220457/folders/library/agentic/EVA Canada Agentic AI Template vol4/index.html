    This Volume is an application guide:
    You will use it to design, describe, and assess AI agent use cases within your department.

    1. Purpose¶

    This Volume provides practical guidance on how to apply the requirements and operating model defined in:

    • Volume 1 – Platform & Agentic Requirements, and
    • Volume 3 – LiveOps & Governance Model,

    to concrete Government of Canada use cases.

    It helps departments answer:

    • What kinds of agents are appropriate for federal work?
    • Which patterns are low-, medium-, or high-risk?
    • How do we map use cases to autonomy levels and lifecycle tiers?
    • What templates can teams use to propose and describe agents?

    2. Scope¶

    2.1 In Scope¶

    • Common agent archetypes suitable for GC departments.
    • Design patterns and anti-patterns for safe agent use.
    • Mapping of use cases to:
    • Autonomy levels (Advisory, Assistive, Semi-Autonomous).
    • Lifecycle tiers (Sandbox, Pilot, Production).
    • Practical templates and checklists:
    • For program teams to propose agents.
    • For governance teams to review them.

    2.2 Out of Scope¶

    • Platform technical requirements (Volume 1).
    • Engineering practices and CI/CD (Volume 2).
    • Detailed LiveOps procedures (Volume 3).

    This Volume uses those; it does not repeat them.

    3. Agent Archetypes for Federal Government¶

    These are generic agent types that appear across departments. Each can be implemented as one or more agents in different project spaces.

    Tags: [CORE] = widely applicable & recommended
    [ADV] = more advanced / higher risk / needs stronger governance

    3.1 Knowledge Explorer (Information Assistant) – [CORE]¶

    Description
    Helps public servants find, navigate, and understand existing information without making recommendations or decisions.

    Typical capabilities:

    • Search across policies, procedures, manuals, FAQs.
    • Highlight relevant sections and definitions.
    • Explain jargon, acronyms, and references in plain language.

    Recommended autonomy level:
    - Advisory only.

    Typical data:

    • Internal documentation, manuals, policies, guidelines.
    • Usually Unclassified or Protected B (depending on context).
    • Normally no direct personal information required.

    Examples (illustrative):

    • Internal “Policy Navigator” for a benefit program.
    • “HR Handbook Explorer” for employees.

    3.2 Drafting Assistant – [CORE]¶

    Description
    Helps draft content for humans to review and finalize.

    Typical capabilities:

    • Drafting internal emails, memos, meeting notes.
    • Drafting letters to clients based on templates.
    • Summarizing documents or meetings.
    • Assisting with translation or bilingual drafting.

    Recommended autonomy level:
    - Assistive – always requires human review before sending/filing.

    Typical data:

    • Work-related content (internal or external communication).
    • May include personal or program information entered by users.
    • Final responsibility remains with the human author.

    3.3 Case Context Summarizer – [CORE / ADV]¶

    Description
    Ingests case-related documents and produces a structured summary for human decision-makers.

    Typical capabilities:

    • Extracting key facts from case files.
    • Chronologically organizing events and evidence.
    • Highlighting missing or conflicting information.

    Recommended autonomy level:
    - Assistive – supports but does not decide.

    Typical data:

    • Personal information in case files.
    • Sensitive program and decision data (often Protected B or higher).
    • Requires strong RBAC, logging, and privacy controls.

    3.4 Process Guide / Triage Assistant – [ADV]¶

    Description
    Helps staff navigate a process or triage tasks based on rules and guidelines.

    Typical capabilities:

    • Asking structured questions to determine which path/queue applies.
    • Suggesting next steps (forms, approvals, routing).
    • Generating an internal recommendation (e.g., route to team X).

    Recommended autonomy levels:

    • Assistive by default:

    • Agent proposes route or next step; human confirms.

    • Semi-Autonomous in limited, low-risk contexts:

    • E.g., routing internal requests that are easy to correct.

    Typical data:

    • Internal process rules and guidance.
    • Sometimes personal/program information used for routing.

    3.5 Decision-Support Advisor – [ADV]¶

    Description
    Provides structured analysis or options in support of complex decisions but does not make decisions.

    Typical capabilities:

    • Highlighting relevant policy sections and case facts.
    • Suggesting questions a decision-maker should consider.
    • Presenting options and trade-offs.

    Recommended autonomy level:
    - Assistive only.
    - No automated decisions affecting rights, benefits, or obligations.

    Typical data:

    • Sensitive personal and program information.
    • Complex, sometimes ambiguous rules and policies.

    3.6 Operational Helper / Automation Agent – [ADV]¶

    Description
    Performs operational actions in systems under strict constraints (low-risk, reversible tasks).

    Typical capabilities:

    • Applying standard labels, tags, or metadata.
    • Creating reminders or tasks based on rules.
    • Triggering standard workflows (e.g., notify internal team).

    Recommended autonomy level:

    • Semi-Autonomous but only for low-risk, easily reversible actions.
    • No changes to eligibility, benefit amounts, or legal statuses.

    Typical data:

    • System metadata, internal reference data.
    • Minimal direct client-facing impact if something goes wrong.

    4. Risk-Aware Design Patterns¶

    This section describes patterns that combine archetypes + autonomy + lifecycle to manage risk.

    4.1 Pattern: “Search + Explain” (Low-Risk) – [CORE]¶

    Use with:
    - Knowledge Explorer.

    Pattern:

    1. User asks a question.
    2. Agent:
    3. Retrieves relevant documents (RAG/search).
    4. Highlights key sections.
    5. Explains in plain language.
    6. Agent never tells user what to decide or do—only provides information.

    Autonomy:
    - Advisory.

    Lifecycle:

    • Appropriate for Sandbox → Pilot → Production with relatively light governance when:
    • Data is low-to-moderate risk.
    • Outputs are clearly labelled “informational”.

    Key safeguards:

    • Clear messaging:

      “This tool provides information only. You remain responsible for any decisions or actions.”

    • Clear source links so users can verify.

    4.2 Pattern: “Draft, Don’t Decide” (Medium-Risk) – [CORE]¶

    Use with:
    - Drafting Assistant, summarization agents.

    Pattern:

    1. User provides context (prompt, documents).
    2. Agent:
    3. Drafts an email, letter, summary, or note.
    4. References sources if applicable.
    5. Human:
    6. Reviews, edits, and approves.
    7. Sends or records the final version.

    Autonomy:
    - Assistive – agent never sends or files content on its own.

    Lifecycle:

    • Start as Sandbox:
    • Experiment with templates and tone using non-sensitive content.
    • Move to Pilot:
    • Limited user groups with training.
    • Production:
    • After demonstration that users understand the need to review content.

    Key safeguards:

    • Explicit reminders in the UI:

      “Always review and edit drafts before sending. You are responsible for the final message.”

    • Logging of draft generation (metadata) for quality reviews.

    4.3 Pattern: “Structured Summary for Human Decision” (Medium-Risk) – [CORE / ADV]¶

    Use with:
    - Case Context Summarizer.

    Pattern:

    1. Agent ingests case documents (within an authorized project space).
    2. Produces:
    3. Chronological event list.
    4. Key facts and indicators.
    5. Links to source documents.
    6. Human decision-maker:
    7. Uses summary as a starting point.
    8. Reads underlying documents.
    9. Records final decision and rationale.

    Autonomy:
    - Assistive only.

    Lifecycle:

    • Sandbox:
    • Use synthetic or de-identified data for early prototypes.
    • Pilot:
    • Use real cases with limited user cohort and strong monitoring.
    • Production:
    • When demonstrated to improve efficiency/quality without replacing human judgment.

    Key safeguards:

    • UI message:

      “Review the original documents before making a decision. This summary is a tool, not the official record.”

    • Strong RBAC and logging (as per Volumes 1 and 3).

    4.4 Pattern: “Recommend, Don’t Route (Yet)” (Medium/High-Risk) – [ADV]¶

    Use with:
    - Process Guide / Triage Assistant.

    Pattern:

    1. Agent asks structured questions.
    2. Uses rules/guidance to recommend a route or next step.
    3. Human:
    4. Confirms, changes, or overrides the suggestion.
    5. Executes the routing or action.

    Autonomy:

    • Start with Assistive.
    • Only consider Semi-Autonomous for:
    • Non-critical routing.
    • Changes easy to detect and reverse.
    • After evidence of reliability and governance approval.

    Lifecycle:

    • Sandbox:
    • Build question flow and logic with mock data.
    • Pilot:
    • Deploy recommendation-only mode.
    • Production:
    • Continue as Assistive or allow limited Semi-Autonomous behaviour on low-risk branches.

    Key safeguards:

    • Log whether recommendations were:
    • Accepted.
    • Overridden (optionally why).
    • Review override patterns regularly.

    4.5 Pattern: “Operational Helper with Guardrails” (High-Risk) – [ADV]¶

    Use with:
    - Operational Helper / Automation Agent.

    Pattern:

    1. Agent performs actions (e.g., tagging, reminders, metadata updates) under strict rules.
    2. Actions are:
    3. Logged.
    4. Visible to humans.
    5. Reversible where feasible.

    Autonomy:
    - Semi-Autonomous – but only for low-risk actions.

    Lifecycle:

    • Sandbox / Pilot:
    • Run in Assistive mode first (human approves actions).
    • Production:
    • Move specific, low-risk actions to Semi-Autonomous after:
      • Strong testing and monitoring.
      • Governance approval.

    Key safeguards:

    • Clear separation between:
    • Low-risk auto-actions (e.g., tags).
    • High-risk actions (e.g., modifying client entitlements) – which remain human-only.
    • Kill switch for the agent or feature.

    5. Anti-Patterns to Avoid¶

    Patterns that are not aligned with this GC template unless heavily constrained and approved.

    5.1 “Black-Box Decision-Maker”¶

    • Agent directly decides:
    • Eligibility.
    • Benefit amount.
    • Sanctions or penalties.
    • No human review, no explanation, no traceability.

    Status:
    - Not acceptable under this template for federal programs.
    - Decisions affecting rights, benefits, or obligations require: - Transparent rules.
    - Human accountability.

    5.2 “Policy Oracle”¶

    • Agent is treated as an authoritative interpreter of law/policy:
    • Users rely on its output as if it were legal advice or official interpretation.
    • No disclaimers; no link back to authoritative sources.

    Status:
    - Must be constrained to Advisory with clear warnings:
    - “Consult official policy or legal counsel for binding interpretation.”

    5.3 “Shadow Agent in Production”¶

    • Team deploys an agent:
    • Outside the approved Platform.
    • Without registration in the Directory.
    • Without LiveOps monitoring or governance review.

    Status:
    - Not acceptable.
    - All agents must: - Be created as project spaces/agents on the Platform.
    - Follow intake, risk assessment, and lifecycle tiers.

    6. Mapping Use Cases to Autonomy & Lifecycle¶

    Use this mapping as a starting point for risk-informed design.

    Use Case Type Archetype Typical Autonomy Starting Tier Likely Target Tier
    Internal policy search Knowledge Explorer Advisory Sandbox Production
    Drafting internal emails/memos Drafting Assistant Assistive Sandbox Production
    Drafting client letters Drafting Assistant Assistive Sandbox Pilot/Production
    Case file summarization Case Context Summarizer Assistive Sandbox Pilot/Production
    Internal workflow triage Process Guide/Triage Assistive Sandbox Pilot/Production
    Auto-tagging documents Operational Helper Semi-Autonomous* Sandbox Pilot/Production
    Decision-support in complex cases Decision-Support Advisor Assistive Sandbox Pilot/Production

    *Semi-Autonomous only for simple, low-risk metadata actions, with strong logging and the ability to undo.

    7.2 Quick Governance Checklist (for Reviews)¶

    For each proposed agent:

    • Purpose & Scope
    • Clear problem statement and benefits.

    • Archetype and pattern selected.

    • Data & Privacy

    • Data sources and classification identified.
    • Personal information use is understood and documented (if applicable).

    • Required privacy assessments identified.

    • Autonomy & Oversight

    • Autonomy level chosen and justified.
    • Human-in-the-loop clearly described.

    • No direct automated decisions affecting rights/benefits (unless covered by separate, explicit automated decision-making framework).

    • Risk & Tier

    • Lifecycle tier (Sandbox/Pilot/Production) selected.
    • Key risks identified and mitigation proposed.

    • Suitable for target tier given risk (e.g., high-risk must start in Sandbox/Pilot).

    • Operational Readiness

    • Logging and monitoring requirements understood.
    • Plan for incident response (including kill switch) exists.

    • Training and AI literacy plan identified for users.

    • Approvals

    • Program/business owner endorsement.
    • AI governance review.
    • Privacy and security review if required.

    7.3 Design Review Questions (For Architecture / AI Governance)¶

    When reviewing a design:

    1. Is an agent the right solution?

    2. Could simple search, forms, or deterministic automation achieve the same outcome?

    3. Is the archetype appropriate?

    4. Are we staying within patterns like “search + explain” or “draft, don’t decide” when risk is high?

    5. Are autonomy and scope constrained?

    6. Are we incrementally adding capabilities or starting with too much power?

    7. Is data use justified and controlled?

    8. Do we really need personal information for this agent?

    9. Are we using the minimum necessary?

    10. Is explainability addressed?

    11. Can users see where information came from?

    12. Are limitations clearly stated?

    13. Is there a realistic plan for monitoring and incident handling?

    14. Who will respond if the agent misbehaves or produces harmful output?
    15. Are kill switches and downgrade paths defined?

    8. Example Journey: From Idea to Pilot¶

    This is a generic journey aligning Volumes 1–3 and this Volume.

    1. Idea & Concept Canvas
    2. Program team fills in the Agent Concept Canvas.

    3. Identifies archetype, pattern, autonomy, and initial tier (e.g., Sandbox).

    4. Initial Review & Intake

    5. AI governance lead and Platform team review:

      • Fit with Platform.
      • Risk level.
      • Required assessments.

    6. Sandbox Implementation

    7. Engineering team implements a minimal version using:

      • Synthetic or de-identified data (if risk is high).
      • Platform components defined in Volumes 1 & 2.

    8. Sandbox Evaluation

    9. Program and governance review:
      • Usefulness and quality.
      • Observed risks or failure modes.

    10. Decide whether to proceed to Pilot.

    11. Pilot Planning

    12. Define:

      • Limited user group.
      • Monitoring, metrics, and feedback loops.
      • Training and AI literacy support.

    13. Pilot Execution

    14. Agent runs in Pilot tier in production-like conditions.

    15. LiveOps (Volume 3) monitors behaviour, usage, and incidents.

    16. Pilot Review

    17. Evaluate:

      • Benefits vs expectations.
      • Incidents and risks.
      • Feedback from users.

    18. Scale or Adjust

    19. Decision options:
      • Scale to broader production.
      • Keep as a targeted pilot.
      • Adjust or retire the agent.

    9. How Volume 4 Connects to Volumes 1–3¶

    • Volume 1 – Platform & Agentic Requirements
    • Provides the rules and constraints (autonomy, RAG, Directory, NFRs).

    • Volume 4 uses those rules to define safe patterns and archetypes.

    • Volume 2 – Engineering & Delivery Guide

    • Defines how we structure code, pipelines, and dev environments.

    • Volume 4 gives engineering teams concrete use-case templates to build.

    • Volume 3 – LiveOps & Governance Model

    • Specifies monitoring, incident management, trust posture, and kill switches.
    • Volume 4 shows how to embed those concerns in use case design and lifecycle.

    10. Tailoring Guidance for Departments¶

    To adopt this Volume:

    1. Pick initial archetypes relevant to your department (e.g., Knowledge Explorer + Drafting Assistant).
    2. Customize templates (Concept Canvas, checklists) with your governance process names and links.
    3. Define a small starter catalogue:
    4. 3–5 target use cases, mapped to archetypes and patterns.
    5. Align with governance bodies:
    6. Use the checklists and review questions as part of AI governance procedures.
    7. Iterate:
    8. Add new archetypes/patterns as experience grows.
    9. Capture lessons learned from pilots into this Volume.
