    Volume 3 – LiveOps & Governance Model (Template)¶

    Subtitle: How We Run, Monitor, and Govern AI Agent Platforms in Production
    Version: Draft 0.2
    Date: YYYY-MM-DD

    A. Instantiation Header (to be filled by each department)¶

    • Department / Organization: {{Department Name}}
    • Platform Name: {{Platform Name (e.g., “Enterprise AI Assistant Platform”)}}

    The rest of the document describes how the Platform is operated and governed once it exists.

    1. Purpose¶

    This Volume defines the operations and governance model (“LiveOps”) for running an internal AI agent platform (the “Platform”) in a Government of Canada context.

    Where:

    • Volume 1 defines what the Platform and agents must do (requirements), and
    • Volume 2 defines how we build it (engineering and delivery),

    Volume 3 defines how we run and govern it once deployed:

    • Operating model, roles, and responsibilities.
    • Environments, SLOs, and reliability expectations.
    • Monitoring, logging, and LiveOps dashboards.
    • Incident, change, and release management (including kill switch).
    • AI-specific governance and risk management (autonomy, privacy, trust posture).

    2. Scope¶

    2.1 In Scope¶

    • Operation of the Platform in non-prod and prod environments.
    • Live monitoring and observability of:
    • Infrastructure and application health.
    • Agent behaviour and usage patterns.
    • Incident and problem management for:
    • Technical issues.
    • Data/privacy issues.
    • Agent behaviour issues (e.g., harmful or misleading outputs).
    • AI-specific governance and risk controls:
    • Autonomy levels and enforcement.
    • Kill switch usage.
    • Trust posture and lifecycle tiers (Sandbox/Pilot/Production).

    2.2 Out of Scope¶

    • Detailed software engineering practices (see Volume 2).
    • Policy drafting and departmental-level AI strategy (refer to GC and departmental policies).
    • Individual program procedures (these plug into this model).

    3. Audience¶

    • Platform Service Owner & Product Owner(s)
    • Platform Operations & Support Teams
    • Program / Project Owners (Solution Owners)
    • AI Governance, Privacy, and Security Officials
    • Enterprise Architects & ITSM / ITIL Leads

    4. Operating Principles¶

    (Each principle tagged as [CORE] – baseline, [ADV] – advanced.)

    1. Service, Not Project
      The Platform is a long-lived service with formal ownership, SLOs, and runbooks—not a one-off project.
      Tag: [CORE]

    2. Shared Platform, Shared Responsibility
      One Platform supports many solutions. Responsibilities for Platform vs solution vs governance must be explicit and documented.
      Tag: [CORE]

    3. Reliability and Safety by Design
      Monitoring, kill switches, and degraded modes are implemented and exercised, not only documented.
      Tag: [CORE]

    4. AI Governed as AI, Not Just Software
      Classic operations (ITIL) are extended with AI-specific controls (autonomy, trust posture, behavioural incidents).
      Tag: [CORE]

    5. Transparency & Traceability
      Significant events (incidents, major changes, autonomy shifts) are logged and explainable after the fact.
      Tag: [CORE]

    6. Continuous Risk Management
      Risk posture is reassessed over time, not just at initial go-live.
      Tag: [ADV]

    5. Operating Model & Roles¶

    5.1 Conceptual Layers¶

    1. Platform Level
      The shared AI backbone:

    2. Agent runtime, RAG services, APIs, logging, monitoring, LiveOps console, Directory, infra.

    3. Solution / Project Level
      Individual project spaces and their agents:

    4. E.g., “Benefits Knowledge Assistant”, “Case Summary Assistant”, “KM Search Assistant”.

    5. Governance Level
      Cross-cutting oversight:

    6. Departmental AI governance bodies.
    7. Privacy office, security, legal, data governance.

    5.2 Key Roles (Template)¶

    Departments SHOULD map these to their own org structure.

    • Platform Service Owner [CORE]

    • Accountable for the Platform as a service:

      • SLOs, roadmap, risk posture, alignment with policy.

    • Platform Operations Team [CORE]

    • Runs the Platform:

      • Monitoring, incident response, deployments, infra maintenance.

    • Solution / Project Owner [CORE]

    • Owns one or more project spaces and agents:

      • Business objectives, data scopes, outcomes and impacts.

    • AI Governance Lead / AI Steward [CORE]

    • Coordinates AI-specific governance:

      • Intake questions, risk reviews, AI compliance tracking.

    • Privacy Officer / Representative [CORE]

    • Advises on personal information use and privacy risks.

    • Security Lead / CISO Representative [CORE]

    • Advises on security classification, threat models, and controls.

    • Support / Service Desk [CORE]

    • First-line contact for user issues and incidents; triages to the right teams.

    Implementation note:
    A separate RACI matrix can be produced as an annex to this Volume, mapping each role to concrete tasks (e.g., “approve autonomy level changes”, “activate kill switch”, “own incident communications”).

    6. Environments, SLOs & Degraded Modes¶

    6.1 Environment Tiers¶

    • Dev – internal testing by Platform and solution teams.
    • Test/QA – integrated testing, including multi-service flows.
    • Stage/UAT – pre-production; near-prod configuration and data (where allowable).

    • Prod – live environment for end-users.

    • R-EnvOps.1 [CORE] – Environment purposes, access, and promotion rules SHALL be documented and enforced.

    6.2 Service Level Objectives (SLOs)¶

    Example SLO areas:

    • Availability

    • E.g., Platform endpoints and UIs target 99.x% availability over a 30/90-day window.

    • Latency

    • P95 latency for core operations (e.g., standard Q&A) under X seconds.

    • P95 latency for heavy RAG operations under Y seconds.

    • Error Rate

    • Acceptable thresholds for 5xx, timeouts, and tool call failures.

    • R-SLO.1 [CORE] – SLOs for critical Platform services SHALL be defined, measured, and regularly reported.

    • R-SLO.2 [ADV] – Where feasible, SLOs SHOULD be tied to error budgets and used to guide release cadence and remediation priorities.

    6.3 Degraded Modes¶

    • R-Mode.1 [CORE] – The Platform SHALL define behaviours for degraded operation, such as:
    • If model endpoint unavailable:
      • Pause intensive operations; show clear user messages.

    • If search index unavailable:

      • Fallback to simpler search or “no RAG results available” messaging.

    • R-Mode.2 [ADV] – Degraded modes SHOULD be:

    • Documented in runbooks.
    • Tested periodically (e.g., through “game days” or drills).

    Implementation note:
    For each major dependency, define: - “Normal” behaviour
    - “Degraded” behaviour
    - “Failure” (kill switch / shutdown) behaviour

    7. Monitoring & LiveOps¶

    7.1 Monitoring Domains¶

    Monitoring SHOULD cover four domains:

    1. Infrastructure & Platform Health

    2. Compute, storage, network, gateways, model endpoints, search/vector stores.

    3. Application & Agent Runtime Health

    4. API response times, error rates, timeouts.

    5. Tool call success/failure metrics.

    6. Usage & Behaviour

    7. Volume of interactions per project/agent.

    8. Usage patterns over time, high usage agents, peak hours.

    9. Governance & Risk Signals

    10. Autonomy level changes.
    11. Kill switch activations.
    12. Guardrail triggers (if implemented).

    13. Policy violation alerts (where technically detectable).

    14. R-Mon.1 [CORE] – The Platform SHALL integrate with departmental monitoring/logging tools (e.g., cloud monitoring + SIEM).

    15. R-Mon.2 [CORE] – Key indicators (availability, latency, error rate) SHALL be captured for all critical services.
    16. R-Mon.3 [ADV] – Governance signals (autonomy changes, kill switch events, trust posture) SHOULD be surfaced on at least one LiveOps dashboard.

    7.2 LiveOps Console / Views¶

    A “LiveOps Console” may be a dedicated UI or a set of dashboards and tools that together provide:

    • Health Views
    • Overall Platform status (green/amber/red).

    • Per-environment status.

    • Agent Views

    • List of agents in production:

      • Project space.
      • Autonomy level.
      • Lifecycle tier (Sandbox/Pilot/Production).
      • Last incident, if any.

    • Usage Views

    • Requests per project/agent.
    • Top agents by usage.

    • User feedback summary (positive/negative flags).

    • Control Panel

    • Per-agent/project kill switch.
    • Links to detailed logs/traces.

    • Links to configuration history (Directory).

    • R-LiveOps.1 [ADV] – A LiveOps console or equivalent SHOULD be available to Platform Ops and governance roles for real-time visibility and control.

    Implementation note:
    Initial implementation can be a set of dashboards in existing monitoring tools + a simple admin UI for kill switches. Over time, these can be consolidated into a more integrated console.

    8. Logging, Tracing & Retention¶

    8.1 Logging Requirements¶

    • R-Log.1 [CORE] – The Platform SHALL log:
    • Interaction metadata (timestamps, project/agent IDs, correlation IDs, success/failure).
    • Tool call metadata (tool name, duration, outcome).

    • Configuration and model version changes.

    • R-Log.2 [CORE] – Logs SHALL be:

    • Secured and access-controlled.

    • Retained according to departmental retention and privacy policies.

    • R-Log.3 [ADV] – Where permitted, sampled or redacted prompt/response content may be logged for QA and improvements, with clear governance and privacy controls.

    8.2 Correlation & Traceability¶

    • R-Trace.1 [CORE] – A correlation ID SHOULD be generated per user interaction and propagated through:
    • Web/UI layer.
    • Agent runtime.

    • Tool calls and downstream services.

    • R-Trace.2 [ADV] – Traces SHOULD enable reconstruction (at a metadata level) of “how an answer was produced” for audit investigations.

    Implementation note:
    Use standard logging formats (JSON preferred) and centralize logs in an existing departmental log platform (e.g., centralized cloud logging + SIEM). Ensure correlation ID is included in each log entry.

    9. Incident & Problem Management¶

    9.1 Incident Types¶

    In addition to standard IT incidents, the Platform recognizes:

    1. Technical Incidents

    2. Outages, performance degradation, API failures, misconfigurations.

    3. Data & Privacy Incidents

    4. Potential misuse of personal information.
    5. Logs or prompts containing data outside the approved classification.

    6. Wrong data sources used by agents.

    7. Agent Behaviour Incidents

    8. Harmful, biased, or significantly incorrect outputs.
    9. Agents acting beyond configured autonomy (“runaway agent” scenarios).

    9.2 Incident Lifecycle¶

    Align with departmental ITSM/ITIL, extended with AI-specific detail:

    1. Detection
    2. Automated alerts (monitoring).
    3. User reports via support channels.

    4. Governance or privacy/security escalation.

    5. Classification & Prioritization

    6. Severity based on:

      • Impact on services / users.
      • Privacy or legal risk.
      • Reputational impact.

    7. Initial Response

    8. Assignment:

      • Platform Ops (infra/runtime).
      • Solution team (agent config/logic).
      • Privacy/Security (for sensitive events).

    9. Containment

    10. Actions could include:

      • Activating kill switch for an agent or project space.
      • Forcing autonomy to a lower level (e.g., Assistive → Advisory).
      • Temporarily disabling certain tools/APIs.

    11. Resolution & Recovery

    12. Technical fixes or configuration changes.
    13. System validations.

    14. Coordinated re-enablement of agents/features.

    15. Post-Incident Review (PIR)

    16. Root cause analysis (RCA).

    17. Identification of:

      • What went well / what did not.
      • Follow-up actions (technical, governance, training).

    18. R-Inc.1 [CORE] – Incident handling SHALL integrate with the department’s existing IT incident management processes, with additional categories for AI/privacy/behavioural incidents.

    19. R-Inc.2 [ADV] – For recurring issues, problem management SHOULD be used to address underlying causes.

    10. Communication & Status Reporting¶

    10.1 Operational Communications¶

    • R-Com.1 [CORE] – For major incidents and planned maintenance impacting users, standard departmental communication channels SHALL be used, such as:
    • IT service management notifications.
    • Internal status pages.

    • Targeted emails / collaboration messages to affected groups.

    • Communications SHOULD:

    • Describe:

      • Impacted services.
      • Severity and business impact.
      • Workarounds, if any.
    • Provide:
      • Regular updates until resolution.
      • Final summary (with any user actions needed).

    10.2 Governance & Executive Reporting¶

    • R-Com.2 [ADV] – On a regular cadence (e.g., quarterly), the Platform Service Owner SHOULD report to governance bodies on:
    • Availability and reliability vs SLOs.
    • Adoption/usage metrics by program/agent.
    • Summary of incidents (especially privacy or behavioural incidents).
    • Changes in autonomy levels or data scope for key agents.
    • Trust posture and risk highlights.

    Implementation note:
    Use existing executive reporting templates (e.g., “Service Health” or “Risk Dashboard”) and add AI-specific sections.

    11. AI Governance, Risk & Trust Posture¶

    11.1 Trust Posture¶

    • R-Trust.1 [ADV] – The Platform SHOULD maintain a trust posture per project/agent, informed by:
    • Governance completeness (intake, assessments, approvals).
    • Operational signals (incidents, error rates, guardrail triggers).
    • Autonomy level and data sensitivity.

    Trust posture may be expressed as:

    • Qualitative: Green / Amber / Red with explanation, or
    • Quantitative: A simple score or index, if a scoring framework is adopted.

    11.2 Governance Lifecycle¶

    • Sandbox – Experimental, clearly labelled; heavily constrained data; not used for operational decisions.
    • Pilot – Limited users, monitored in LiveOps; partial governance completed.

    • Production – Fully governed and integrated into normal workflows.

    • R-Trust.2 [CORE] – Lifecycle tier (Sandbox/Pilot/Production) SHALL be recorded for each project/agent and visible in the Directory and LiveOps dashboards.

    11.3 Governance Bodies & Approvals¶

    • R-Trust.3 [CORE] – High-risk changes (e.g., increased autonomy, new use of personal information, decision-support in sensitive domains) SHALL require review and approval by relevant governance bodies (AI, privacy, security, program).

    Implementation note:
    Map “high-risk” triggers to departmental AI governance frameworks (e.g., thresholds in risk self-assessment, presence of personal data, impact on benefits/rights).

    12. Autonomy Management & Kill Switch¶

    12.1 Autonomy Management¶

    • R-Auto.1 [CORE] – Each agent SHALL have a documented autonomy level (Advisory, Assistive, Semi-Autonomous) and associated guardrails.
    • R-Auto.2 [CORE] – Changes to autonomy level SHALL be treated as high-impact configuration changes and:
    • Require explicit approval by Project Owner + AI Governance (and Privacy/Security where applicable).
    • Be logged (who, what, when, why).

    12.2 Kill Switch¶

    • R-Kill.1 [CORE] – A kill switch SHALL exist at least at the agent and project-space level to:
    • Immediately disable the agent from responding to user requests, and/or

    • Restrict it to a safe fallback message (e.g., “This assistant is temporarily unavailable.”).

    • R-Kill.2 [CORE] – Kill switch actions MUST:

    • Be restricted to authorized roles (Platform Ops, AI Governance/Steward, Security).

    • Be logged with actor identity, time, target (agent/project), and reason.

    • R-Kill.3 [ADV] – Departments SHOULD periodically test kill switch functionality in non-production environments (and, where feasible, simulate scenarios in production with minimal impact).

    Implementation note:
    Implement kill switch as a simple, robust mechanism first (config flag + routing rules) before adding sophisticated automation. Reliability and clarity are more important than elegance.

    13. Change & Release Management (AI-Aware)¶

    13.1 Change Types¶

    • Standard Changes

    • Low risk, repeatable (e.g., minor UI fixes, non-breaking config tweaks).

    • Normal Changes

    • Typical features and non-critical infra modifications.

    • Major / High-Risk Changes (AI)

    • New agents for sensitive domains.
    • Changes to autonomy levels or data classification.
    • Model version changes with material behavioural differences.

    • New accesses to personal or sensitive data.

    • R-Chg.1 [CORE] – High-risk changes SHALL:

    • Be explicitly identified in change records.
    • Include risk assessment and mitigation steps.
    • Involve relevant governance bodies (AI, privacy, security, program).

    13.2 Pre-Release Checks¶

    • R-Chg.2 [CORE] – Before releasing to production, high-risk changes SHALL be:
    • Tested in appropriate pre-prod environments.
    • Supported by updated runbooks (operations and incident handling).
    • Accompanied by communication to Platform Ops and, where necessary, end-users.

    13.3 Post-Release Monitoring¶

    • R-Chg.3 [ADV] – For significant changes, departments SHOULD:
    • Define a “heightened monitoring” period post-release.
    • Capture early metrics and feedback to detect unexpected behaviour.

    14. Privacy & Personal Information Oversight¶

    14.1 Personal Information Handling¶

    • R-Priv.1 [CORE] – The Platform SHALL obey departmental rules on:
    • When and how personal information can be used.
    • Which project spaces/agents may handle personal information.

    • Retention and masking requirements.

    • R-Priv.2 [CORE] – Where personal information is in scope:

    • Privacy assessments and approvals (PIA or equivalent) MUST be completed as required.
    • Access to logs or replay functionality MUST be carefully controlled and minimized.

    14.2 Privacy Controls¶

    Examples (may be technical or procedural):

    • Input guidance and warnings when handling real client data.
    • Restrictions on exporting logs or transcripts.

    • Aggregated usage reporting for privacy review (without exposing raw personal data).

    • R-Priv.3 [ADV] – Departments SHOULD implement mechanisms to:

    • Flag potential privacy incidents.
    • Review patterns in aggregate (e.g., data categories used, common failures).

    15. Documentation, Runbooks & Training¶

    15.1 Operational Runbooks¶

    • R-Run.1 [CORE] – Runbooks SHALL exist for:
    • Common technical incidents (model endpoints, search, infra).
    • Kill switch activation and rollback.
    • Degraded mode activation.

    • Escalation paths for privacy/behaviour incidents.

    • R-Run.2 [ADV] – Runbooks SHOULD be version-controlled and tested (tabletop exercises or non-prod drills).

    15.2 Governance Playbooks¶

    • R-Gov.1 [ADV] – Governance playbooks SHOULD describe:
    • The steps from Sandbox → Pilot → Production for project spaces.
    • Required assessments and approvals at each stage.
    • Thresholds for risk that trigger additional oversight.

    15.3 Training¶

    • R-Train.1 [CORE] – Training materials SHALL be available for:
    • Platform Ops and Support teams (LiveOps tools, kill switch, incident handling).
    • Solution/Project Owners (autonomy levels, governance expectations).
    • End-users (responsible use, limitations, feedback mechanisms).

    Implementation note:
    Align with broader AI literacy initiatives and departmental awareness campaigns; reference Volume 4 for use-case-oriented training content.

    16. Tailoring Guidance for Departments¶

    When adopting this Volume, departments SHOULD:

    1. Fill the Instantiation Header (Section A).
    2. Map template roles to real org roles (Directorates, Branches).
    3. Define environment names and SLOs aligned with departmental standards.
    4. Decide which monitoring tools and dashboards will form the LiveOps console.
    5. Integrate AI incidents and behavioural issues into existing ITSM processes.
    6. Define “high-risk” change criteria in alignment with:
    7. Departmental AI guidelines.
    8. Privacy and security policies.
    9. Establish governance hooks:
    10. AI governance, privacy, and security review triggers.
    11. Reporting cadence to executive committees.

    17. Relationship to Other Volumes¶

    • Volume 1 – Platform & Agentic Requirements
      Defines what must be observable and controllable in production (logs, autonomy, lifecycle tiers, governance). Volume 3 shows how to operate and govern those capabilities once deployed.

    • Volume 2 – Engineering & Delivery Guide
      Provides build-time practices (CI/CD, logging hooks, dev containers) that make LiveOps feasible. Volume 3 assumes those practices exist and explains how to use them operationally.

    • Volume 4 – Use Cases, Patterns & Templates
      Describes agent archetypes, patterns, and journeys from idea to pilot/production that must be governed under this LiveOps model.
